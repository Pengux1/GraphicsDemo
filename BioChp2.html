Question 1

List at least five types of proteins, state their functions, and give an example of each.

Motility - Allow movement of cells and their organelles - Actin and myosin work together to move muscles.

Neurotransmitters - Signalling between neurons - Endorphins activate nerve receptors to alleviate pain or stress.

Enzymes - Catalyse biochemical reactions - DNA polymerase, an enzyme that duplicates DNA.

Transport - Carry molecules from one location to another or across cell membranes - Haemoglobin carries oxygen to body cells.

Structural - Provide support, strength and protection - Collagen supports body tissues.

Question 2

How does an amino acid get its name?

An amino acid always contains an amino group and a carboxyl group(Carboxylic acid) within it's structure, the term being a blend of the two.

Question 3

Distinguish between hydrophilic and hydrophobic amino acids. Suggest where each is likely to be found in a folded protein and explain why.

Hydrophilic amino acids contain a polar/charged R group, while hydrophobic amino acids contain a non-polar R group. A hydrophilic amino acid is likely to be found within the protein molecule, away from the water in the aqueous external enviroment, while a hydrophilic amino acid is likely to be found on the surface of a protein due to their affinity for water

Question 1

Describe how amino acids are linked together.

Amino acids bond together in the process of condensation polymerisation. Each bond between two adjacent amino acids is called a peptide bond

Question 2

Describe the four levels of protein structure.

Primary - Determined by DNA, this is the sequence of amino acids in the polypeptide.

Secondary - Once the polypeptide chain is formed, various parts undergo folding and coiling due to hydrogen bonding between the peptide bonds of neighbouring amino acids. These portions from the secondary strucutre.

Tertiary - Hydrophilic and Hyrdrophobic R groups attract like groups, causing the polypeptide chain to become folded, coiled or twisted into the proteins functional shape or conformation. This determines the function of the protein.

Quaternary - This forms when two or more polypeptides associate into the mature protein.

Question 3

What three types of folds are associated with a polypeptide's secondary structure?

α-helices(Tight Coils), β-pleated sheets(Flattened folding forms) and random loops.

Question 4

Explain what is meant by the term 'denature' and relate the term to a protein's function.

Denature means to destroy the characteristic properties of (a protein or other biological macromolecule) by heat, acidity, or other effect which disrupts its molecular conformation. If a protein denatures, it will be unable to do it's original function

Question 5

Explain the benefits of proteomics.

Proteomics allows for the study of the proteins and how they interact with each other in a specific tissue. How this changes in diseased tissue can be used to determine the mechanism by which the disease works.

Question 1

Describe the three key features of a nucleotide and how they are arranged.

A nucleotide has a five-carbon sugar(Deoxyribose in DNA), a negatively charged phosphate group, which gives DNA an overall negative charge and an organic nitrogen-containing compound called a base. The carbons of the sugar are numbered 1' to 5', the nitrogenous base is attached to carbon 1' of the sugar, the phosphate group is attached to carbon 5' of the sugar.

If DNA structure is described as 'antiparallel' what does this mean?

The 5' end of one strand comes together with the 3' end of the complementary strand.

Question 5

What charge does DNA have, and why?

Question 1

Identify three fundamental differences between the structures of DNA and RNA.

The deoxyribose sugar of the DNA and the ribose sugar of RNA differ in that deoxyribose has one less oxygen atom at carbon 2'. The nitrogenous base Thymine(T) is replaced by Uracil(U). RNA tends to be single stranded while DNA is doubled stranded.

Question 2

Define two key processes in gene expression.

Two key processes in gene expression are: Transcription - The copying of the DNA into RNA; and Translation - The conversion of the nucleotide sequence in the mRNa, into the amino acid sequence of a polypeptide.

Question 3

List three major types of RNA and describe their functions.

mRNA - Messenger RNA which conveys the instructions needed for protein synthesis from the DNA in the nucleus to the cytoplasm where the proteins are made.

rRNA - Ribosomal RNA which acts a structural molecule for the formation of the ribosome.

tRNA - Transfer RNA which exist as free floating molecules in the cytoplasm, which bring in the amino acids into the ribosome.

Question 3

Define exons and introns, and explain how they relate to the processing of pre-mRNA.

Exons contain the information for polypeptide formation, while Introns are regions in the exons that 'interupt' the coding sequences. Both are present in pre-mRNA, but introns are removed and and exons rejoined by RNA splicing before the mRNA leaves the nucleus.

Question 4

Describe how alternative splicing generates different mRNA transcripts.

During RNA processing, different exons may be removed along with the introns to produce mRNA molecules of different length and sequence.

Question 5

What are the 5' and 3' caps, and what do they achieve for the mature mRNA?

The 5' cap is a methylated cap at the 5' end, and the 3' cap is a poly-A tail added by Polyadenylation. These protect the mature RNA from degredation.

Question 1

Explain what is meant by a 'dengenerate' genetic code.

A degenerate genetic code is one which has redundant sections, allowing for most amino acids to be encoded by two or more codons.

Question 2

An anticodon is a sequence of three bases in the tRNA that are complementary to a codon in the mRNA.

Question 3

How does the lac repressor block transcription?

The lac operon produces a repressor protein, which binds to a region of non-coding DNA called an operator, blocking RNA from transcribing the three genes of the lac operon.

Question 4

How does lactose switch on gene expression at the lac operon?

Lactose binds to the repressor, altering its shape so it cannot bind to the operator, allowing the operon to be transcribed.

Question 5

What benefit is there to the cell by regulating gene expression of the lac operon?

It prevents the wasting of resources that would be used to create the unused lactose handling proteins when there is no lactose in the cell.
