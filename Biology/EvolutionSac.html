A definition of natural selection and all of the steps.

Natural Selection is the process whereby organisms better adapted to their enviroment tend to survive and produce more offspring.

The steps of Natural Selection are:

  1. Individuals undergoe mutations to their unique genome, sometimes expressing themselves as variations.
  2. Over time, individuals with variations that are more suited to the enviroment are more likely to survive, and thus reproduce.
  3. These genes that allowed the individual to be successful are passed to the offspring, inherited by the next generation.

An understanding of the processes used to establish genetic relatedness.

Basically any Homology can be used to establish relatedness. Some techniques that can be used are:

Molecular Homology, where the similarity of nucleotide sequences in DNA or amino acid sequences in different species can be used to determine relatedness.

Mitochondrial DNA(mtDNA) is often used as a marker for genetic relatedness. As it is in almoost all eukaryotic cells, has a large enough and sufficently varible sequence and is inherited materinally, with a high mutation rate, the differences between species can be used to determine relatedness.

Molecular Clock is the number of subsitutions that have accumlated in the amino acid sequence of a given nucleotide or polypeptide in a given lineage.

DNA Hybridisation is the technique where two different DNA samples are incubated to form hybrid double strands, then heated to seperation. The more closely related the dna sequences, the higher the energy and thus temperature needed to seperated the strands.

An understanding of population genetics including speciation, gene flow, genetic drift and the founder effect.

Speciation, caused by Divergent Evolution, is the formation of new and distinct species in the course of evolution.

Gene Flow the transfer of alleles from one population to another. Migration into or out of a population may be responsible for a marked change in allele frequencies. Increases genetic variation.

Genetic Drift is the random changes that occur in gene pools over time, due to chance effects.

Understand the threats to survival associated with a lack of variation.

Lack of genetic variation reduces a species ability to evolve in response to a changing enviroment, which may cause an increased risk of extinction. As a species population dwindles, it loses genetic variation, only to be restored slowly through the accumulation of mutations over many generations. Lack of variation can also increase the risk of lethal phenotypes, caused by recessive traits.

Understand when and why a lack of variation is not important.

If an enviroment is stable(ie. No Selection Pressures), variations will not typically be necessary for a species survival.

All of the steps involved with speciation.

  1. Seperation occurs, from geological sources, enviromental change, resource scarcity, etc.
  2. Adaptation occurs, where the members of this newly isolated species undergo selection, natural or sexual, causing changes to their gene pool.
  3. Reproductive Isolation occurs, the changes between the two populations acumulating to a point where they are no longer able to interbreed and produce viable, fertile offspring.

Adaptive Radiation occurs when a single species eventually gives rise to a whole new group of organisms comprising many new species.

Allopatric Speciation is when gene flow is disrupted as populations become physically seperated through geographical isolation, leading the populations to diverge and undergo speciation.

Sympatric Speciation is the evolution of two or more new species from a single population within the same place.

Pre-reproductive Isolating Mechanisms include: Geographic Mechanisms, time mechanisms, bechavioural mechanisms.

Post-reproductive Isolating Mechanisms don't prevent mating, but do prevent young from being produced, including: Gamete mortality, Zygote mortality and Hybrid sterility.

Be aware of gene flow and the effect of gene flow on variation, speciation and gene pool stability/change.

Gene flow causes variation to propegate through sucessive populations, allowing speciation to occur, and leading to gene pool change.

Define the term founder effect.

The Founder Effect is when indiduals that move to a new area and become isolated might not carry all the alleles that were present in the original population

Understand how the founder effect influences variation in a population.

The Founder Effect can lead to less genetic diversity/variation in the new population.

Define the term population bottleneck.

Population bottleneck is a sharp reduction in the size of a population due to enviromental events ir human activities.

Understand how population bottlenecks influence variation in a population.

It causes a decrease in the size of the population and thus, decreases variation in a population.

Understand how population bottlenecks are associated with genetic drift.

The Genetic Drift caused by a population bottleneck changes the distribuation of alleles by chance and even lead to fixation or loss of alleles.

Be able to construct and analyse cladograms (phylogenetic trees).

Cladograms are a type of phylogenetic trees that represent a hypothesis for the evolutionary history leading to descendant species. Each branch or clade represents an ancestor and all its decendants.

Each node represents the last common ancestor of the lineages that emerge from it. The tree is represented over a function of time.

Be aware of the difference between convergent and divergent evolution and associate homologous and analogous structures with these processes.

Convergent Evolution is the process where different organisms not closely related independantly devlop similar traits to adapt to a similar enviroment/niche.

It is related to Analgous Structures, structures that have similar functions but different structure and evolutionary history. Examples of Analgous Structures are: Wings in flying animals such as bats and birds, Eyes in animals such as octopi and humans, and

Divergent Evolution is the accumulation of differences between groups that can lead to the formation of new species.

It is related to Homologus Structures, structures that have different functions but the same structure. Examples of Homologus Structures are: The arm of a human, wing of a bat and flipper of a whale are homologous structures.
