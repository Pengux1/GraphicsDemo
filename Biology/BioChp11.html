Summary

Genetic Engineering

Genetic Engineering refers to changing the genetic sequence of an organism through human use of modern biotechnology techniques.

Common gene modifications include:

  1. Cutting out genes or gene segments to prevent expression or proper function (Knockout organisms).
  2. Inserting genes into a specific locus so that they are controlled by a particular promoter in the organisms' genome (Knock-in Organism).
  3. Inserting DNA from one organism into the genome of another unrelated organism in a non-specific locus (Transgenic Organism).

Polymerase Chain Reaction(PCR)

PCR is a bioengineering technique used to increase amounts of DNA in a sample. It makes use of the enzyme DNA Polymerase, which catalyses the formation of new DNA molecules in the nucleus from free nucleotides.

The components needed for PCR are:

  • The DNA to be copied.
  • DNA polymerase.
  • A buffer solution that contains salts and other chemicals to help the DNA polymerase function.
  • A supply of the four nucleotides from which to build the new DNA molecules.
  • Two primers, a short sequence (Around 20 nucleotides) of single stranded DNA, complementary to the nucleotide sequences at either end of the DNA section that is to be copied.

PCR has three steps:

  1. Denaturation, where the DNA is heated to 95 C causing the two strands to denature.
  2. Annealing, where the temperature is reduced to 60 C, and primers are added, allowing them to join to the strands.
  3. Extension, where the temperature is raised to 72 C, which allows the DNA Polymerase to replicate the DNA.

Each cycle of PCR doubles the amount of DNA molecules in the sample.

Restriction Endonucleases

Cutting DNA at know sequences is done using Restriction Endonucleases, which cut DNA into smaller pieces named Restriction Fragments in a controlled way.

Restriction enzymes only cut DNA at specific sequences, known as Restriction sites or Recognition Sequences. Most recognition sites are palindromes of their complementary sequence.

Restriction Enzymes occur naturally in bacteria, used as a dfence against bacteriophages (viruses), and are thus named after the bacterial strain from which they are derrived.

Restriction enzymes produce either Sticky Ends, which leave nucleotides exposed, or Blunt Ends, in which the cut occured at the same posistion in each strand of the DNA and there are no overlapping strands. For genetic modification purposes, sticky ends are prefered as they are more selective than blunt ends.

Plasmids

Plasmids are circular pieces of DNA found in bacteria which reproduce independently to the bacterial chromosome. Plasmids are the ideal vectors for DNA fragments as they are copied many times within the cell, and are copied when bacteria replicate. Their circular shape makes them much more stable than linear DNA, and can easily carry many different genes.

DNA Ligase

DNA Ligase is an enzyme used to join different pieces of DNA together, joining them by the backbone of the DNA. The success rate of joining two fragments of DNA together is vastly improved if the two strands are brought together somehow.

Essentially, DNA Ligase is like DNA sticky tape.

Gel Elctrophoresis

Gel Electrophoresis is a technique that seperates fragments of DNA according to their size and charge.

DNA fragments are put into an agarose gel, which is placed in a tray filled with buffer solution, with positive and negative electrodes attached to each end of the gel. When the electic current runs through the gel, the DNA fragments are repelled from the negative electrode and move towards the positive end. Smaller fragments are able to wriggle through the gel faster, therefore seperating the DNA by their size.

A fluroescent DNA-binding dye such as ethidium bromide, allowing the seperated DNA to be seen.

Genetic Cloning: Copying DNA using Plasmids

An alternative to PCR is to insert it into bacteria. The process is Gene cloning and allows the replication of larger segments of DNA and permits the analysis of any gene in the DNA sequence.

Plasmids are used to insert DNA into the bacteria, involving a number of steps.

  1. Plasmids are extracted from bacteria by ruptering the cell walls.
  2. The same restriction enzyme is used to cut the plasmid DNA and the DNA of the gene to be inserted so that both pieces of DNA have complementary sticky ends.
  3. DNA ligase binds to the 'foreign' DNA fragment into the plasmids DNA. After binding, the DNA fragment becomes a permanent part of the Recombinant Plasmid.
  4. The recombinant plasmids are added to a bacterial culture. They are taken up by some bacteria, in which they replicate. In the normal process of growth and division, bacteria replicate the plasmid, and thus numerous copies of the incorperated foreign DNA are made.

Only a small percentage of the bacteria take up the recombinant plasmids, the process of bacteria taking up plasmids called Transformation.

Plasmid DNA often contains genes for resistance to an antibiotic, used in Antibiotic Selection to kill bacteria that have not been transformed and thus, have the antibiotic resistance.

Biotechnology Applications

Gene Therapies can be used for:

  • Identifying alleles of genes responsible for serious medical conditions in an effort to speed up the discovery of a treatment for them.
  • Screening for genetic diseases to determine if an individual or embryo is a carrier of a genetic disease.
  • Developing transgenic organisms to produce large quantities of protein that can be used for commercial or theraputic purposes.
  • Gene therapy for humans with a genetic disease.

Genetic Sequencing can be used for these purposes.
