Chapter 8

Mechanisms of Evolution

Relevant Study Design

• The qualitative treatment of the causes of changing allele frequencies in a population’s gene pool including types of mutations (point, frameshift, block) as a source of new alleles, chromosomal abnormalities (aneuploidy and polyploidy), environmental selection pressures on phenotypes as the mechanism for natural selection, gene flow, and genetic drift (bottleneck and founder effects) and the biological consequences of such changes in terms of increased or reduced genetic diversity.

• Processes of evolution including through the action of mutations and different selection pressures on a fragmented population and subsequent isolating mechanisms (allopatric speciation) that prevent gene flow.

• The manipulation of gene pools through selective breeding programs.

Question 1

SNPs are common within genomes. Define SNP and describe what type of mutation they usually are if they are so common.

Snps are single nucelotide polymorphisms, which are differences between sequences in the nucleotides at one position. They are usually a point mutation.

Question 2

Desribe the relationships between:

a. Haploid and Diploid.

Haploid is one set of homologous chromosomes (n), Diploid is two sets of homologous chromosomes (2n).

b. Monoploid and Haploid.

Monoploid is having one set of homologous chromosomes, and is thus Haploid.

c. Monoploid, diploid and polypoid.

Monoploid is one set of homologous chromosomes, Polyploid is when an organism acquires one or more complete sets of chromosomes, and can happen when a haploid and a diploid gamete fuse.

d. Diploid and aneuploid.

Aneuploidy is the condition in which there is an addition or loss of one chromosome from a cell.

Question 4

Define 'population' and 'gene pool'.

A population is a group of individuals of the same species that live in the same geographic area and readily interbreed to produce fertile offspring. A gene pool is the total collection of alleles within a population.

Question 1

Outline the meaning of the following and give an example of each.

a. Natural Selection.

Favourable traits are selected for, inherited and become more common in subsequent generations. An example of this is moths whos colour blends into the enviroment producing more offspring with that colour than those who stand out.

b. Sexual Selection.

A form of selection through which individuals with certain inherited characteristics or behaviours are more likely than others to obtain mates and pass on their genes. An example of this is peacocks with larger tails getting more mates.

Question 1

What is the biological species concept?

Species are groups of actual, or potentially, interbreeding natural population that are reproductively isolated from other such populations.

Question 2

Recall the morphological species concept and state when it is used.

This concept identifies different sepcies based on their physical and physiological characteristics, but is limited to what can be observed in the fossil record.

Question 1

List four examples of pre-reproductive isolating mechanisms.

  1. Geographic Mechanisms: individuals are seperated by geographic features.
  2. Temporal Mechanisms: Individuals breed during different seasons of the year, or different times of day.
  3. Behavioural Mechanisms: Individuals have different courtship patterns.
  4. Morphological Mechanisms: Individuals have different reproductive structures.

Question 2

List three examples of post-reproductive isolating mechanisms.

  1. Gamete Mortality: The gametes do not survive.
  2. Zygot Mortality: The zygote forms but does not survive.
  3. Hybrid sterility: Adult offspring are formed but are infertile because they are unable to produce viable gametes.

Question 3

A hybrid organism is a organism produced by two different species mating.
