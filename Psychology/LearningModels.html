Relevant Study Design

• Classical conditioning as a three-phase process (before conditioning, during conditioning and after conditioning) that results in the involuntary association between a neutral stimulus and unconditioned stimulus to produce a conditioned response, including stimulus generalisation, stimulus discrimination, extinction and spontaneous recovery.

• Operant conditioning as a three-phase model (antecedent, behaviour, consequence) involving reinforcers (positive and negative) and punishment (including response cost) that can be used to change voluntary behaviours, including stimulus generalisation, stimulus discrimination and spontaneous recovery (excluding schedules of reinforcement).

• Observational learning as a method of social learning, particularly in children, involving attention, retention, reproduction, motivation and reinforcement.

• The ‘Little Albert’ experiment as illustrating how classical conditioning can be used to condition an emotional response, including ethical implications of the experiment.

Summary

Learning

Learning is defined as a relatively permanent change in behaviour that occurs as a result of experience. It is an ongoing process that continues throughout the lifespan, allowing the ability to asapt and cope in a changing enviroment.

Learning can occur intentionally or unintentionally, and can be active or passive, and is defined as relatively permanent due to it not having to produce a permanent lifelong change.

Conditioning is the process of learning associations between a stimulus in the enviroment and a behavioural response, and has two main types, Classical and operant.

Classical Conditioning

Also known as Respondent Conditioning, Classical Conditioning refers to a type of learning that occurs through the repeated association of two or more different stimuli. Learning is said to only have taken place when a stimulus consistently produces a response that it did not previously produce. Learning results from the involuntary linking of this stimulus, over a number of trials, with a stimulus that normally produces the response automatically. In Classical Conditioning, a stimulus is any object or event that elicits a response from an organism. A response is a reaction by an organism to a stimulus.

Classical Conditioning is often described as a three phrase learning process - Before conditioning, during conditioning and after conditioning. These terms are described by the following five key terms:

Unconditioned Stimulus (UCS)

Any stimulus that consistently produces a particular, naturally occuring, automatic response.

Unconditioned Response (UCR)

The response that occurs automatically when the UCS is presented. A UCR is a reflexive involuntary response that is predictably caused by a UCS.

Neutral Stimulus (NS)

Any stimulus that does not normally produce a predictable response. In particular, this stimulus is 'neutral' to the UCR.

Conditioned Stimulus (CS)

The stimulus that is 'neutral' at the start of the conditioning process but eventually triggers a very similar response to that cuased by the UCS.

Conditioned Response (CR)

The learned response that is produced by the CS. The CR occurs after the NS has been associated with the UCS and has become a CS. The behaviour involved in a CR is very similar to that of the UCR, but is triggered by the CS alone.

During classical conditioning,each paired presentation of the NS with the UCS is reffered to as a trial. The term acquistion is used to describe the overall process during which an orgranism learns to associate two events. During acquistion, the NS and UCS should be presented close together in time, and always in the same seqeuence. The duration of the acquisition stage is measured by the number of trials it takes for the CR to be learned, and is often very fast early in the acquistion period.

The NS should be shown before the UCS and there should be a very shory time between their presentations, no more than half a second. The longer the time interval, the less effective it is to establish a link.

Stimulus Gerneralisation

Stimulus Generalisation is the tendency for another stimulus that is similar to the original CS to produce a response that is similar, but always identical, to the CR. The greater the similarity between stimuli, the greater the possibility that a generalisation will occur. While stimulus generalisation is rarely an intentional or even a conscious process, it has a valuable adaptive role, but can become maladaptive depending on the context.

Stimulus Discrimination

In classical conditioning, stimulus discrimination occurs when an organism responds to the CS only, but not to any other stimulus that is similar to the CS.

Extinction

A conditioned reponse is not necessarily permanent. The strength of the association may fade over time or dissapear altogether. Extinction is the gradual decrease in the strength or rate of a CR that occurs when the UCS is no longer presented. It is said to have occured when a CR no longer occurs following presentation of the CS. The rate at which Extinction occurs varries, but in general, the more complex the behaviour pattern, the longer it will take.

Spontaneous Recovery

Extinction of a conditoned response is not always permanent. Spontaneous Recovery is the reappearance of a CR when the CS is presented, following a rest period after the CR apperas to have been extinguished. It does not always occur, and tends to be short lived and weaker then the inital response. If extinction is repeated several times, spontaneous recovery will not occur.

These definitions are for Classical Conditioning, and are different in terms of operant conditioning.

Little Albert

An emotional reaction in response to a specific stimulus can be learnt through classical conditioning, called a conditioned emotional response. This occurs when the autonomic nervous system produces a reaction to a stimulus that it previously didn't. This can be used to develop harmful maladaptive fears, as illustrated by the Little Albert Experiment.

The experiment was designed to test the belief that fears can be acquired through classical conditioning, through the use of a sole human participant, Albert B. (Little Albert). The infant was particulary docile, and did not regularly cry, making it an excellent candidate for the experiment. After testing him to ensure he could develop a fear response, the experiment began.

Albert was placed on a mattress in a room where a white laboratory rat (CR) was placed within reaching distance. Albert showed no inital fear, and happily played with the animal. As Albert was playing with the white rat, one of the experimenters distracted him, while the other stood behind Albert and struck a hammer against a suspended steel bar, causing a loud noice (UCS), that started Albert, causing flailing of his limbs and shortened increased breath sounds. These reactions grew more pronounced and intense in nature as this was further repeated, on the third round reducing Albert to tears.

Encouraged by this, a further series of fear-conditioning procedures was conducted with Albert, held irregulary over a 17-day period. Where the following experimental procedures were followed:

White rat was taken from basket and presented to Albert, he reaches for the rat with his left hand, and just as he touches the animal, the bar was struck imediately above Alberts head. This caused him to jump violently, and to bury his head in the mattress. As he reached out with his right hand, the bar was struck again, causing him to fall and whimper. One week later, this was repeated again until 7 pairings were made. Albert showing a distinct fear response (CR) in response to the rat (CS) being placed near him. The researchers discovered that Alberts nearly acquired fear response also generalised to other stimuli that were similar to the rat, such as a white fluffy dog.

Ethical Implications of the Experiment

Albert was never removed of his conditioned emotional responses by the inital reasearches, and it is believed that the mother was not fully aware of the experiment, and how her son was to be used. This violates informed consent. It is also not clear if Albert was given clear withdrawal rights, and notes from the experiment suggest that Albert was possibl not a suitable research participant. Albert would have also experienced considerable emotional harm, violating beneficence. Because of this, this experiment is considered unethical by modern standards.

Operant Conditioning

Operant Conditioning is a type of learning whereby the consequences of behaviour determine the likelihood that it will be performed again in the future. Operant conditioning proposes that an organism will repeat a behaviour that has desirable consquences, or avoid undesirable consequences.

An Operant is any response that operates on the enviroment to produce some kind of consequence. Positive consequences strengthen the behaviour and make it more likely to recur and adverse consequences weaken the behaviour and make it less likely to recur. Since the consequence occurs in the environment, the environment determines whether or not the operant occurs. Unlike classical conditioning, operant conditioning involes voluntary responses.

Operant Conditioning as a Three-Phase Model

This model states that all behaviour can be analysed and explained by the relationship between the behaviour, its antecedents and its consequences. This three way relationship is called the three-phase model of operant conditioning.

The three phase model of operant conditioning has three parts that occur in a specific sequence, which are:

  1. Antecdent - A stimulus that occurs before the behaviour.
  2. The Behaviour - That occurs due to the antecedent.
  3. The Consequence - To the behaviour.

Anything in the organism's enviroment can be an antecedent, and are already in place before any behaviour occurs. When an antecedent does influence the likelihood of specific behaviour occursing, it is called an antecedent stimulus. The antecedent stimulus must be present for the relevant behaviour to occur. Through its association with a consequence, the antecedent stimulus signals whether certain behaviour will lead to a particular consequence. The antecedent stimulus is sometimes referred to as the antecent condition to emphasise that it occurs before the relevant behaviour.

Behaviour is the voluntary action that occurs in the presence of the antecedent stimulus. It may be one specific action or a pattern of actions. In all cases, it involves activity that has an efect on the enviroment in the form of a consequence that follows it.

Consequence is the enviromental event that occurs immediately after the behvaiour and has an effect on the occurrence of the bahviour. Any behaviour which is followed by a consequence will change in strength (become more, or less, established) and frequency (occur more, or less, often) depending on the nature of that consequence (reward or punishment). Behaviour that is followed by a reward strengthens the behaviour and makes it more likely to occur again, whereas behaviour followed by punishment weakens the behaviour and makes it less likely to occur again.

Reinforcers

Reinforcement is said to occur when a stimulus strengthens or increases the frequency of a response that follows. This may invoilve using a postive stimulus, or a removing a negative one. An essential feature of reinforcement is that it is only used after the desired response is made. A reinforcer is any stimulus that increases the frequency of a response that follows. Reinforcer and reward are used interchangeably.

Positive Reinforcer

A positive reinforcer is a stimulus that increases the frequency of a desired response by providing a satisfying consequence. Positive Reinforcement involves giving a positive reinforcer after the desired response has been made.

Negative Reinforcer

A negative reinforcer is any unpleasnt stimulus that whe removed or avioided, increases the frequency of a desired response. Negative Reinforcement involves the removal or avoidance of an unpleasant stimulus. It has the effect of increasing the likelihood of a response being repeated, strengthing the reponse. An important distinction between positive and negative reinforcement is that positive reinforcers are given and negative reinforcers are removed or avoided.

Punishment

Punishment is the delivery of an unpleasant consequence following a response, or the removal of a pleasant consequence following a response. Punishment has the same unpleasant quality as a negative reinforcer, but unlike a negative reinforcer, the punishment is given or applied, whereas the negative reinforcer is prevented or avoided.

Positive Punishment involves the presentation of a stimulus, thereby decreasing the likelihood of a response occuring again. Negative Punishment involves the removal of a stimulus and thereby decreasing the likelihood of a response occuring again. In both positive and negative punishment, the intended effect on the punished behaviour is to weaken and prevent it from recurring.

Response Cost

Since negative punishment involves taking a stimulus away as a consequence of a particular response, it is often referred to as response cost. Reponse cost may be described as involving removal of any valued stimulus, whether or not it causes the behaviour. There is a cost for making a response, therefore 'response cost'.

Stimulus Generalisation

Occurs when the correct response is made to another stimulus that is similar to the stimulus that was present when the conditioned response was reinforced. The response usually occurs at a reduced level.

Stimulus Discrimination

Occurs when an organism makes the correct response to a stimulus, even when stimuli are similar (but not identical).

Extinction

The gradual decrease in stength of a conditioned response following consistent non-reinforcement of the response. Extinction is said to have occurred when a conditioned response is no longer present. Extinction occurs over time after reinforcement is no longer present.

Spontaneous Recovery

After extinction, the organism can once again show the response in the absence of any reinforcement. This response is likely to be weaker and will not last as long. A spontaneously recovered response is often stronger when it occurs after a lengthy period following extinction of the response than when it occurs relatively soon after extinction.

A model is who or what is being observed, and may be live or symbolic. As observational learning involves watching models, it is often called modelling.

Observational Learning Processes

Attention

To learn through observation, one must pay attention to a model's behaviour and the consequences. Attention can be influenced by perceptual capabilites of the observer, the motivation and interest level of the observer, the situation in which the behaviour is being observed, the kinds of distracters that are present and the characteristics of the model, such as attractiveness.

In general, the greater the similarity between the model and the observer, and the more attractive or successful the model, the more likely we are to follow their example.

Retention

After observing the model, you have to remember the behaviour. Behaviour learned is often not needed until much later, requiring it to be stored in memory properly.

Reproduction

When a model's behaviour has been closely watched, and retained in memory, one can attempt to reproduce what has been observed. To do this, you must be able to have the ability to properly reproduce the behaviour.
