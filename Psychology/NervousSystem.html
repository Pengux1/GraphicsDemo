Nervous System Functioning

Chapter 1

Relevant Study Design

• The roles of different divisions of the nervous system (central and peripheral nervous systems and their associated sub-divisions) in responding to, and integrating and coordinating with, sensory stimuli received by the body.

• The distinction between conscious and unconscious responses by the nervous system to sensory stimuli, including the role of the spinal reflex

• The role of the neuron (dendrites, axon, myelin and axon terminals) as the primary cell involved in the reception and transmission of information across the synapse (excluding details related to signal transduction)

• The role of neurotransmitters in the transmission of neural information between neurons (lock-and-key process) to produce excitatory effects (as with glutamate) or inhibitory effects (as with gamma amino butyric acid [GABA])

• The effects of chronic changes to the functioning of the nervous system due to interference to neurotransmitter function, illustrated by the role of GABA in Parkinson’s disease.

Summary

Divisions of the Nervous System

Central Nervous System

The Central Nervous System(CNS) comprises of the Brain and its extension, the spin. Its main function is to process information received from the body's internal and external enviroments and to activate appropirate responses.

Brain

An intricate network of cells that play a vital role in processing information received through neural pathways from the body and in directing actions within the body. It continuously receives and analyses sensory information, responding by controlling all bodily actions and functions. It can be considered the control center of the body as it regulates all bodily functions. Brain ceslls are organised into many identifiable areas and structures that have specialised functions.

Spine

A long, thin budle of nerve tissue that extends from the base of the brain to the lower back. The spinal cord links the brain and the parts of the body below the neck. Its two primary functions are to:

  1. Receive sensory infromation from the body and send them to the brain.
  2. Receive motor information from the brain and send it to relevant parts of the body.

Peripheral Nervous System

The Peripheral Nervous System(PNS) is the entire network of nerves located outside the CNS. It extends to all parts of the body, and has the primary function of transmiting information to and from the CNS. The PNS acheives this through the two following subdivisions.

Autonomic Nervous System

The Autonomic Nervous System(ANS) is a subdivision of the PNS that connects the CNS to the body's internal organs and glands, providing feedback to the brain about their activities. It is refered to as 'autonomous' because many of the organs, glands and processes under its control are self-regulating and therefore occur without conscious effor, and are not under voluntary control. The ANS functions continuously, regardless of ones level of awareness, but however is not completely self-regulating. The ANS also has two subdivisions, as follows.

Sympathetic Nervous System

The Sympathetic Nervous System is a subdivision of the PNS that activates internal muscles, organs and glands to prepare the body for vigorous activity or to deal with a stressful or threatening situation. It is activated by a stressor or fear stimulus and enhances survival by providing an immediate response, in a split second, to any kind of emergency.

The Sympathetic Nervous System has many effects on the body, including but not limited to:

• Heart rate, blood pressure and breathing rate increasing to allow for greater oxygen supply to muscles.

• Pupils dilate(Expand) to allow more light to go into the eye, thus allowing for better vision.

• Digestion is slowed down, to reduce the amount of energy the body is using.

Parasympathetic Nervous System

The Parasympathetic Nervous System is a subdivision of the PNS that helps to maintain the internal body enviroment in a steady, balanced state of normal functioning, in times of minimal stress and in the absence of threat. Generally, it has the function of counterbalancing the activites of the Sympathetic Nervous System, restroing the body to a state of calm once the need for the sympathetic nervous system has passed.

The Parasympathetic Nervous System dominates the Sympathetic Nervous System most of the time, and generally does the reverse of the Sympathetic Nervous System.

Somatic Nervous System

The Somatic Nervous System(SNS) is a network of nerves that carry sensory information to the CNS, and motor information from the CNS. Sensory information is received at sensory receptor sites(Duh), and carried along sensory neural pathways by sensory neurons. motor information is carried along motor neural pathways by motor neural pathways by motor neurons(Again, Duh) to skeletal muscles to control their activity by causing them to contract or relax. Skeletal muscles are attached to our bones and respond to messages from the CNS to initiate, change or stop movement.

Sensory Information is called Afferent, and motor information Efferent. This can be remembered as SAME, Sensory Afferent, Motor Efferent.

Basically, say that that the SNS is responsible for voluntary skeletal muscle movement.

Conscious and Unconscious Responses to Stimuli

Simply enough, a Conscious Response to a stimulus is a reaction that involves awareness. You will have paid attention to the stimulus and therefore know about it, and therefore can be described as voluntary and goal directed, thus allowing for some kind of control over it.

Shockingly, an Unconscious Response to a stimulus is a reaction that does not involve aweness.It is involuntary, unintentional, automatic and we cannot ordinarily control its occurrence. Bodily responses regulated by the ANS occur automatically without conscious effort, many of which are reflexive responses.

Spinal Reflex

A spinal reflex is an unconscious, involuntary and automatically occurring response to certain stimuli without any involvement of the brain. It is often referred to as a reflex arc because the response to an incoming stimulus is automatically ‘reflected back’ from the spinal cord without any initial input from the brain and before the brain processes a conscious perception of the stimulus.

Absence of various spinal reflexes can indicate neural disorders.

The spinal reflex demonstrates that a response to a particular sensory stimulus can have both an unconscious and conscious component, one occurring before the other.

Role of the Neuron

A neuron is an individual nerve cell that is specialised to receive, process and/or transmit information. Neurons not only communicate with each other, but also with muscles and glands. The entire nervous system is comprised of neurons organised into networks that form neural circuits and pathways of varying complexity through which information is continuously transmitted. They carry information in the form of an action potential, either sending it to the appropirate part of the nervous system, or interpreting the message, enabling a response.

Parts of the Neuron

Dendrites

An extension of a neuron that detects and receives information from other neurons. Most dendrites have little protrusions, or ‘outgrowths’, called dendritic spines. Each spine provides a site with receptors where a neuron can connect with and receive information from a neighbouring neuron. The neuron’s capability to form (‘grow’) new dendritic spines is associated with and demonstrates neuroplasticity. When the dendrites receive information from other neurons, they pass it on to the neuron’s soma (cell body) where it is integrated. The soma may collect and integrate information from thousands of other neurons. Once the incoming information from other neurons has been integrated in the soma, it is transmitted along the axon.

Axon

A single, tubelike, extension that transmits neural information to other neurons, sometimes having branches allowing a message to be sent to multiple cells.

Myelin

A white, fatty substance made of certain types of glial cells, that surrounds and insulates the axon. The coating, called the myelin sheath allows an action potential to travel down the axon, without interference from other nearby axons; also allowing messages to travel much faster without being interrupted or distorted.

Axon Terminals

At the end of an axon, there are small branches called axon collaterals, at the end of which are axon terminals. Each axon terminal has a terminal button, a small sac like structure that stores and scretes neurotransmitters made by the neuron.

Role of Neurotransmitters

Neurons communicate with each other by sending neurotransmitter across the tiny space between the terminal buttons of one neuron, and the dendrites of the other, called the synaptic gap. The synaptic gap is a component of the synapse, the site where communication occurs betwen to adjacent neurons.

Neurotransmitter is a chemical substance produced by a neuron that carries a message to other neurons or cless in muscles, organs or other tissue. Neurotransmitters work by binding to receptor sites of postsynaptic neurons that are specialised to recieve that spcific neurotransmitter. A neurotransmitter that does not bind to the recpetors in the postsynaptic neuron is reabsorbed into the neuron by a process called reuptake.

Generally, a neurotransmitter will wither excite or inhibit neuron firing. Glutamate is the primary excitatory neurotransmitter in the CNS, making postsynaptic neurons more likely to fire. It is associated with enhanced learning and memory. GABA is the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter in the CNS, making neurons less likely to fire. GABA works to to control and fine tune neuron firing, to prevent it from getting out of control.

Neurotransmitters only fit to certain receptors through a lock and key process, where a neurotransmitters shape must match the shape of the receptor site, allowing the neurotransmitters specific function to occur.

Parkinson's Disease

Parkinson's is a CNS neurodegenerative disorder characterised by both motor and non-motor symptoms. Motor symptoms result from the degeneration of the substantia nigra located in the midbrain, the site at which dopamine is produced in the brain. Dopamine from the substantia nigra carries messages on how to control body movements, and thus diminished levels of it are responsible for most of the symptoms of this disease.

Four key symptoms are used for diagnosis of Parkinsons. These are:

  • • Tremor - Continuous, involuntary shaking.
  • • Muscle Rigidity - Muscles are unable to relax, and are tight, even at rest.
  • • Slowness of Voluntary Movement(Bradykinesia) - Particually when initating and executing movement, and in performing repetitive movements. Also a decrease in fine motor coordination, such as being able to do up buttons on a shirt.
  • • Postural Instability, Balance Problems and Gait (Walking) disturbances - Inability to maintain a steady, upright posture or to take a corrective action to prevent a fall often results in just that — falling. Gait disturbance is apparent in the short, shuffling steps taken by individuals and reduced arm swing.

There is no known cure for Parkinsons, but motor symptoms can be relieved by by medications that restore the deficiency of dopamine by increasing its level in the brain. Two types of medication can be used, those that can be converted into dopamine by neurons(Levodopa), and those that mimic the role of dopamine(Bromocriptine, Pramipexole, and Ropinirole). These medications however, provide diminishing effects over time, requiring higher and higher doses to be effective, and can have severe side effects, particularly at higher doses. In some cases, Deep Brain Stimulation may be an option, working by placing an electrode in the basal ganglia(Controls motor function), that essentially works as a filter, stopping excess muscle movement.

Treatment and Symptoms are not explicitly mentioned in the study design, so don't try too hard to memorise this.

There is growing evidence that the midbrain dopamine neurons also release GABA, meaning that there is a reduced level of GABA in the brains of those with Parkinsons. It is belived that dopamine therapy is not completely effective due to the diminished level of GABA not being addressed.
